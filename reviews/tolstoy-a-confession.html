A Confession

by Leo Tolstoy — October 10, 2025

This book is a really interesting addition after having read Tolstoy's diaries. Whereas his diaries are very much concerned with the content of the faith Tolstoy professed towards the end of his life, this book explains, in an autobiographical manner, how he found that faith.

Although he grew up religious, he abandoned his Orthodox Christian faith as a teenager. As a successful and well-off writer in 19th century Russia, he finds himself surrounded by people that are also non-religious and that encourage him to live an immoral life. He feels that this is wrong, as he has a deep desire to do good, but he is being ridiculed when he tries to act upon it. A couple years later he has a deep existential crisis that leads him into a state of despair and to the verge of suicide. After searching for the answers to the question of the meaning of life in the sciences and in philosophy, he reckons that Solomon and Schopenhauer are right and that life is an absurdity and evil. The only logical consequence is that it should be destroyed by suicide, yet he is not able to do this. This creates even more despair for Tolstoy. After a few troubled years he finally realizes that the reason he saw life as meaningless is that his life actually was meaningless, and even the lives of most of the people around him. He understands how the way that he lived in a bubble prevented him to find the truth, which is actually to be found among the working people, that live, day in, day out, without despairing about the meaning of life:
"I instinctively felt that if I wished to live and understand the meaning of life, I must seek this meaning not among those who have lost it and wish to kill themselves, but among those milliards of the past and the present who make life and who support the burden of their own lives and of ours also."

What he finds there is, of course, religion - a faith in the existence of God which gives those people meaning in their life. Now here is the bit that I found maybe the most interesting in the book:

"Whatever the faith may be, and whatever answers it may give, and to whomsoever it gives them, every such answer gives to the finite existence of man an infinite meaning, a meaning not destroyed by sufferings, deprivations, or death. This means that only in faith can we find for life a meaning and a possibility … faith is a knowledge of the meaning of human life in consequence of which man does not destroy himself but lives. Faith is the strength of life. If a man lives he believes in something. If he did not believe that one must live for something, he would not live. If he does not see and recognize the illusory nature of the finite, he believes in the finite; if he understands the illusory nature of the finite, he must believe in the infinite. Without faith he cannot live."

I can't help but see this concept of faith closely related to Paul Tillichs ultimate concern (According to Tillich, faith is a centered act of being ultimately concerned). He also says that the content of faith does not matter for the definition of faith, which fits the indefiniteness of "one must live for something" in Tolstoy quite well.

And then this wonderful passage:
"'What more do you seek?' exclaimed a voice within me. 'This is He. He is that without which one cannot live. To know God and to live is one and the same thing. God is life.' 'Live seeking God, and then you will not live without God.' And more than ever before, all within me and around me lit up, and the light did not again abandon me."

What happens next? After initially embracing the Christian Orthodox faith from his childhood again fully, he grows more and more doubtful about the religious institutions and the rites: "And I ceased to doubt, and became fully convinced that not all was true in the religion I had joined … But where did the truth and where did the falsehood come from? Both the falsehood and the truth were contained in the so-called holy tradition and in the Scriptures. Both the falsehood and the truth had been handed down by what is called the Church."
And so begins his rebellion against the institutionalized religion which finally lead to his excommunication from the Russian Orthodox Church (although this is not part of the book).
His disentangling of the truths and the falsehoods of the Christian faith are also not to be found in this book, but it gets quite obvious from his diaries and maybe also from other books of his that I haven't read yet.
